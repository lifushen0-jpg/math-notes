## Q1. 
Recall Problem 6 from the last assignment, we call a linear operator satisfying $T^*T = TT^*$ a normal operator. We further discuss some properties of normal operators.

   (a) A linear operator $B$ on $V$ is called skew if
   
   $$
   B^* = -B.
   $$
   
   Let $T$ also be a linear operator on $V$. Prove that $T$ is normal if and only if there exist commuting operators $A$ and $B$ such that $A$ is self-adjoint, $B$ is a skew operator, and $T = A + B$.

   (b) Let $T$ be a linear operator on $V$, and for any $v \in V$, $\|T^*v\| \le \|Tv\|$. Prove that $T$ is normal.

   (c) Let $P$ be a linear operator on $V$ such that $P^2 = P$. Prove the following are equivalent:
   (i) $P$ is self-adjoint.
   (ii) $P$ is normal.
   (iii) There exists a subspace $U$ of $V$ such that $P = P_U$.

   (d) For a normal linear operator $T$ on $V$, prove that:
   
   $$
   \ker T^k = \ker T \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Im } T^k = \text{Im } T
   $$

   (e) Let $T$ be a normal linear operator on $V$. Prove that if $\lambda \in F$, then the minimal polynomial of $T$ is not a polynomial multiple of $(x - \lambda)^2$.

### Solutions

(a)

We prove both directions.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Assume that $T$ is normal, so $T^*T = TT^*$.
Define

$$
A = \frac{T + T^*}{2}, \qquad B = \frac{T - T^*}{2}.
$$

Then $T = A + B$. Also,

$$
A^* = \frac{T^* + T}{2} = A, \qquad B^* = \frac{T^* - T}{2} = -B,
$$

so $A$ is self-adjoint and $B$ is skew.

Now compute

$$
AB = \frac{1}{4}(T+T^*)(T-T^*)
= \frac{1}{4}(T^2 - TT^* + T^*T - (T^*)^2),
$$

and since $T^*T = TT^*$,

$$
AB = \frac{1}{4}(T^2 - (T^*)^2).
$$

Similarly,

$$
BA = \frac{1}{4}(T-T^*)(T+T^*)
= \frac{1}{4}(T^2 + TT^* - T^*T - (T^*)^2)
= \frac{1}{4}(T^2 - (T^*)^2).
$$

Hence $AB = BA$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, assume that $T = A + B$, where $A^* = A$, $B^* = -B$, and $AB = BA$.
Then

$$
T^* = A^* + B^* = A - B.
$$

Therefore,

$$
T^*T = (A-B)(A+B) = A^2 + AB - BA - B^2,
$$

and

$$
TT^* = (A+B)(A-B) = A^2 - AB + BA - B^2.
$$

Because $AB = BA$, these two expressions are equal. Thus $T^*T = TT^*$, so $T$ is normal.

(b)

Assume that for every $v \in V$,

$$
\|T^*v\| \le \|Tv\|.
$$

Squaring both sides gives

$$
\|T^*v\|^2 \le \|Tv\|^2.
$$

Using the inner product,

$$
\langle T^*v, T^*v \rangle \le \langle Tv, Tv \rangle,
$$

that is,

$$
\langle TT^*v, v \rangle \le \langle T^*Tv, v \rangle.
$$

Hence

$$
\langle (TT^* - T^*T)v, v \rangle \le 0
\qquad \text{for all } v \in V.
$$

Let

$$
B = TT^* - T^*T.
$$

Then $B^* = B$, so $B$ is self-adjoint. The inequality above shows that $B$ is negative semidefinite, hence all eigenvalues of $B$ are $\le 0$.

On the other hand,

$$
\operatorname{tr}(B) = \operatorname{tr}(TT^*) - \operatorname{tr}(T^*T) = 0.
$$

Since $B$ is self-adjoint, its trace is the sum of its eigenvalues. Because all eigenvalues are non-positive and their sum is $0$, all eigenvalues must be $0$. Therefore $B = 0$, so

$$
TT^* = T^*T.
$$

Thus $T$ is normal.

(c)

We prove that (i), (ii), and (iii) are equivalent.

$(i) \Rightarrow (ii)$.
If $P$ is self-adjoint, then $P^* = P$, so

$$
P^*P = P^2 = PP^*.
$$

Thus $P$ is normal.

$(ii) \Rightarrow (iii)$.
Assume that $P$ is normal and $P^2 = P$.
Then the minimal polynomial of $P$ divides

$$
x^2 - x = x(x-1).
$$

So the only possible eigenvalues are $0$ and $1$.
Because $P$ is normal, it is unitarily diagonalizable. Hence there is an orthonormal basis in which the matrix of $P$ is diagonal with diagonal entries only $0$ and $1$.

Let $U = \operatorname{Im} P$, equivalently the eigenspace corresponding to the eigenvalue $1$.
Then $V = U \oplus U^\perp$, and $P$ acts as the identity on $U$ and as $0$ on $U^\perp$.
Therefore $P$ is exactly the orthogonal projection onto $U$, that is,

$$
P = P_U.
$$

$(iii) \Rightarrow (i)$.
Assume that $P = P_U$ for some subspace $U$ of $V$.
Then for any $x, y \in V$, write

$$
x = u_1 + w_1, \qquad y = u_2 + w_2,
$$

where $u_1, u_2 \in U$ and $w_1, w_2 \in U^\perp$.
Since $P_U x = u_1$ and $P_U y = u_2$,

$$
\langle Px, y \rangle = \langle u_1, u_2 + w_2 \rangle = \langle u_1, u_2 \rangle,
$$

and

$$
\langle x, Py \rangle = \langle u_1 + w_1, u_2 \rangle = \langle u_1, u_2 \rangle.
$$

So $\langle Px, y \rangle = \langle x, Py \rangle$ for all $x, y \in V$, hence $P$ is self-adjoint.

Therefore (i), (ii), and (iii) are equivalent.

(d)

First note that for any linear operator,

$$
\ker T \subseteq \ker T^2 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq \ker T^k.
$$

So it is enough to prove that $\ker T^2 = \ker T$.

Let $x \in \ker T^2$. Then $T^2x = 0$, so $Tx \in \ker T$.
Since $T$ is normal, we have

$$
\|Ty\| = \|T^*y\| \qquad \text{for all } y \in V,
$$

which implies $\ker T = \ker T^*$.
Therefore $Tx \in \ker T^*$, so

$$
T^*Tx = 0.
$$

Now

$$
\|Tx\|^2 = \langle Tx, Tx \rangle = \langle T^*Tx, x \rangle = 0.
$$

Hence $Tx = 0$, so $x \in \ker T$.
Thus $\ker T^2 \subseteq \ker T$, and therefore

$$
\ker T^2 = \ker T.
$$

Repeating the same argument for $T^2, T^3, \dots$, or by induction, we obtain

$$
\ker T^k = \ker T.
$$

For the image, we always have

$$
\operatorname{Im} T^k \subseteq \operatorname{Im} T.
$$

By rank-nullity,

$$
\dim(\operatorname{Im} T^k) = \dim V - \dim(\ker T^k)
= \dim V - \dim(\ker T)
= \dim(\operatorname{Im} T).
$$

Since $\operatorname{Im} T^k \subseteq \operatorname{Im} T$ and the two subspaces have the same dimension, they are equal:

$$
\operatorname{Im} T^k = \operatorname{Im} T.
$$

(e)

Let $m_T(x)$ be the minimal polynomial of $T$.
Because $T$ is normal on a complex inner product space, $T$ is unitarily diagonalizable.
Hence the minimal polynomial of $T$ has no repeated roots.

If $m_T(x)$ were divisible by $(x-\lambda)^2$ for some $\lambda \in F$, then $\lambda$ would be a repeated root of the minimal polynomial, which is impossible.

Therefore the minimal polynomial of $T$ is not divisible by $(x-\lambda)^2$.

## Q3.

(a) For a normal operator $T$ on $V$, prove that if an operator $S$ commutes with $T$, then it also commutes with $T^*$.

(b) Let $F = \mathbb{C}$ and $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \operatorname{End}(V)$. Prove that $V$ has an orthonormal basis such that every element in $\mathcal{E}$ has a diagonal matrix with respect to it, if and only if for all $S, T \in \mathcal{E}$, $S$ and $T$ are commuting normal operators.

(c) Let $F = \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \operatorname{End}(V)$. Prove that $V$ has an orthonormal basis such that every element in $\mathcal{E}$ has a diagonal matrix with respect to it, if and only if for all $S, T \in \mathcal{E}$, $S$ and $T$ are commuting self-adjoint operators.

### Solutions

**Lemma**: If a matrix $A$ is diagonalizable, then $AB = BA \iff \exists P \in GL_n(\mathbb{C})$ such that $PAP^{-1}$ and $PBP^{-1}$ are both diagonal matrices. 
*(In other words, diagonalizable commuting operators share a common eigenbasis).*

- **For (a) (a special case of Fuglede's Theorem)**: Since $T$ is normal, it is necessarily diagonalizable. By the lemma, $S$ and $T$ share a common eigenbasis. Furthermore, an eigenvector of a normal operator $T$ is necessarily an eigenvector of $T^*$ (with the complex conjugate eigenvalue). Therefore, both $S$ and $T^*$ act as diagonal matrices (scalar multiplication) on this basis, making them commute obviously.

- **For (b) and (c)**: The sufficiency ($\Rightarrow$) is obvious (diagonal matrices naturally commute, and under an orthonormal basis, they satisfy the normal/self-adjoint conditions respectively). For necessity ($\Leftarrow$), the lemma tells us these commuting operators share a common eigenspace decomposition. Combined with the core property of normal/self-adjoint operators — *eigenspaces corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are mutually orthogonal* — we only need to apply Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization within each common eigenspace and concatenate them to obtain an orthonormal basis for the entire space, under which all operators are diagonal.


## Q4,5
The Review of SVD 

### KEY
Suppose $T \in \operatorname{Hom}(V, W)$ be a linear map, $\dim V = n$, $\dim W = m$, and $A$ is its matrix representation under the standard bases. 

We want to find orthogonal matrices $U \in O(m)$ and $V_0 \in O(n)$ (using $V_0$ to avoid confusion with the space $V$), such that $A = U\Sigma V_0^T$, where $\Sigma$ is a diagonal matrix containing the singular values.

By the spectral theorem, since $AA^T$ and $A^TA$ are symmetric, we have:
$$\exists U \in O(m): AA^T = U\Lambda_1 U^T$$
$$\exists V_0 \in O(n): A^TA = V_0\Lambda_2 V_0^T$$
where $\Lambda_1$ and $\Lambda_2$ share the same non-zero elements.
Then $A = U\Sigma V_0^T$ where $\Sigma = \operatorname{diag}\{\sqrt{\lambda_i}\}$ is what we want.

Geometric representation of singular value decomposition: Suppose $U = (u_1, u_2, \cdots, u_m)$ and $V_0 = (v_1, v_2, \cdots, v_n)$. Then we can write $A$ as a sum of rank-1 matrices:
$$A = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$
Applying this to a vector $x \in V$:
$$Ax = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T x = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i \langle v_i, x \rangle u_i$$
where $\langle -, - \rangle$ denotes the inner product.

**How to obtain the geometric version directly without using matrices?**

For a linear operator $T \in \mathcal{L}(V, W)$, we want to find an orthonormal basis $\{e_1, \dots, e_n\}$ for $V$ and an orthonormal basis $\{f_1, \dots, f_m\}$ for $W$ such that $T$ maps each $e_i$ to a scalar multiple of $f_i$.

1. **Construct a self-adjoint operator**: Consider $T^*T \in \mathcal{L}(V, V)$. This operator is self-adjoint ($(T^*T)^* = T^*T$) and positive semi-definite ($\langle T^*Tv, v \rangle = \|Tv\|^2 \ge 0$).
2. **Apply the Spectral Theorem**: Since $T^*T$ is self-adjoint, by the spectral theorem, there exists an orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, \dots, e_n\}$ of $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T^*T$. Let the corresponding eigenvalues be $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n \ge 0$. We define the **singular values** as $s_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$. Suppose the first $r$ singular values are strictly positive.
3. **Construct the basis for $W$**: For $i = 1, \dots, r$, define:
   $$ f_i = \frac{Te_i}{s_i} $$
   We can verify that these form an orthonormal set in $W$:
   $$ \langle f_i, f_j \rangle = \frac{1}{s_i s_j} \langle Te_i, Te_j \rangle = \frac{1}{s_i s_j} \langle T^*Te_i, e_j \rangle = \frac{s_i^2}{s_i s_j} \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij} $$
   *(We can then extend $\{f_1, \dots, f_r\}$ to an orthonormal basis $\{f_1, \dots, f_m\}$ for the whole space $W$.)*
4. **The Geometric Formula**: For any vector $v \in V$, we can expand it using the basis $\{e_i\}$: $v = \sum_{i=1}^n \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i$. Applying $T$ to both sides (and noting that $Te_i = 0$ for $i > r$):
   $$ Tv = \sum_{i=1}^r \langle v, e_i \rangle Te_i = \sum_{i=1}^r s_i \langle v, e_i \rangle f_i $$
This gives us the elegant, matrix-free geometric representation of SVD.

## Q6.
Let $T \in \operatorname{End}(V, W)$, and let
$$
s_1 \ge \cdots \ge s_m
$$
be the positive singular values of $T$. Assume that $1 \le k < m$. Prove that
$$
\min\{ \|T-S\| : S \in \operatorname{End}(V, W),\ \operatorname{rank} S \le k \} = s_{k+1}.
$$

Moreover, if
$$
Tv = s_1 \langle v, e_1 \rangle f_1 + \cdots + s_m \langle v, e_m \rangle f_m
$$
is a singular value decomposition of $T$, and if $T_k \in \operatorname{End}(V, W)$ is defined by
$$
T_k v = s_1 \langle v, e_1 \rangle f_1 + \cdots + s_k \langle v, e_k \rangle f_k
$$
for every $v \in V$, then
$$
\operatorname{rank} T_k = k
$$
and
$$
\|T - T_k\| = s_{k+1}.
$$

### Proof

From the singular value decomposition,
$$
Tv = \sum_{i=1}^m s_i \langle v, e_i \rangle f_i,
$$
where $\{e_1, \dots, e_m\}$ is an orthonormal set in $V$ and $\{f_1, \dots, f_m\}$ is an orthonormal set in $W$.

We first prove the lower bound. Let $S \in \operatorname{End}(V, W)$ satisfy
$$
\operatorname{rank} S \le k.
$$
We will show that
$$
\|T-S\| \ge s_{k+1}.
$$

Set
$$
E = \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \dots, e_{k+1}\}.
$$
Then $\dim E = k+1$. Since $\operatorname{rank} S \le k$, we have
$$
\dim S(E) \le k.
$$
Hence the restriction
$$
S|_E : E \to W
$$
cannot be injective. Therefore there exists a nonzero vector $x \in E$ such that
$$
Sx = 0.
$$
After normalization, we may assume that $\|x\|=1$. Since $x \in E$, we can write
$$
x = \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} \alpha_i e_i,
\qquad
\sum_{i=1}^{k+1} |\alpha_i|^2 = 1.
$$
Then
$$
Tx = \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} s_i \alpha_i f_i,
$$
so
$$
\|Tx\|^2
= \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} s_i^2 |\alpha_i|^2
\ge s_{k+1}^2 \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} |\alpha_i|^2
= s_{k+1}^2.
$$
Since $Sx=0$, it follows that
$$
\|(T-S)x\| = \|Tx\| \ge s_{k+1}.
$$
By the definition of the operator norm,
$$
\|T-S\|
= \max_{\|y\|=1} \|(T-S)y\|
\ge \|(T-S)x\|
\ge s_{k+1}.
$$
Because this holds for every $S$ with $\operatorname{rank} S \le k$, we obtain
$$
\min\{ \|T-S\| : \operatorname{rank} S \le k \} \ge s_{k+1}.
$$

Now we show that the truncated singular value decomposition attains this bound. Define
$$
T_k v = s_1 \langle v, e_1 \rangle f_1 + \cdots + s_k \langle v, e_k \rangle f_k.
$$

First, we prove that $\operatorname{rank} T_k = k$. For $1 \le i \le k$,
$$
T_k e_i = s_i f_i.
$$
Since each $s_i > 0$, the vectors $T_k e_1, \dots, T_k e_k$ are nonzero scalar multiples of $f_1, \dots, f_k$, hence they are linearly independent. Therefore
$$
\dim \operatorname{Ran}(T_k) \ge k.
$$
On the other hand, by definition,
$$
\operatorname{Ran}(T_k) \subseteq \operatorname{span}\{f_1, \dots, f_k\},
$$
so
$$
\dim \operatorname{Ran}(T_k) \le k.
$$
Thus
$$
\operatorname{rank} T_k = k.
$$

Next, for any $v \in V$,

$$
(T-T_k)v
= s_{k+1} \langle v, e_{k+1} \rangle f_{k+1}
+ \cdots +
s_m \langle v, e_m \rangle f_m
$$

Hence
$$
\|(T-T_k)v\|^2
= \sum_{i=k+1}^m s_i^2 |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2.
$$
If $\|v\|=1$, then
$$
\sum_{i=k+1}^m |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2 \le 1,
$$
and therefore
$$
\|(T-T_k)v\|^2
\le s_{k+1}^2 \sum_{i=k+1}^m |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2
\le s_{k+1}^2.
$$
So
$$
\|T-T_k\| \le s_{k+1}.
$$

To get the reverse inequality, take $v=e_{k+1}$. Then $\|v\|=1$ and
$$
(T-T_k)e_{k+1} = s_{k+1} f_{k+1},
$$
which implies
$$
\|T-T_k\| \ge \|(T-T_k)e_{k+1}\| = s_{k+1}.
$$
Consequently,
$$
\|T-T_k\| = s_{k+1}.
$$

Combining the lower bound with the construction of $T_k$, we conclude that
$$
\min\{ \|T-S\| : S \in \operatorname{End}(V, W),\ \operatorname{rank} S \le k \} = s_{k+1},
$$
and the minimum is attained by $T_k$.

### Remark

When dealing with
$$
\min_S \max_{\|x\|=1} \|(T-S)x\|,
$$
one should keep the order of quantifiers clear. For the lower bound, we first fix an arbitrary $S$ with $\operatorname{rank} S \le k$, then choose a unit vector $x$ depending on this fixed $S$ so that $\|(T-S)x\| \ge s_{k+1}$. Only after obtaining a bound valid for each such $S$ do we take the minimum over all $S$. This is exactly why the argument works.

## Q1.

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space.

(a) Prove that every element of $(V^*)^*$ is of the form
$$
\langle \cdot, x \rangle : f \mapsto \langle f, x \rangle := f(x)
$$
for some $x \in V$. Hence obtain the canonical isomorphism
$$
V \cong (V^*)^*.
$$

(b) Via the canonical isomorphism
$$
x \mapsto \langle \cdot, x \rangle,
$$
identify $(V^*)^*$ with $V$. Prove that for every basis
$$
\phi_1, \dots, \phi_n
$$
of $V^*$, there exists a basis
$$
v_1, \dots, v_n
$$
of $V$ such that $\phi_1, \dots, \phi_n$ is the dual basis of $v_1, \dots, v_n$.

(c) Using the canonical isomorphism above, prove that for every linear map $\phi$ on $V$, the double dual map $(\phi^*)^*$ corresponds to $\phi$; equivalently,
$$
(\phi^*)^* \cong \phi.
$$

(d) Let $\phi$ be a linear operator on the finite-dimensional vector space $V$. For
$$
f \in (\ker \phi)^\perp,
$$
if
$$
\phi^2 = \phi,
$$
prove that
$$
\phi^* f = f.
$$

### Solutions

(a)

Define
$$
\iota : V \to (V^*)^*
$$
by
$$
\iota(x)(f)=f(x), \qquad x\in V,\ f\in V^*.
$$
This map is linear.

We first show that $\iota$ is injective. If $\iota(x)=0$, then
$$
f(x)=0 \qquad \text{for all } f\in V^*.
$$
If $x\ne 0$, we can extend $\{x\}$ to a basis of $V$ and define a linear functional $f$ such that $f(x)=1$, a contradiction. Hence $x=0$.

Since $V$ is finite-dimensional,
$$
\dim (V^*)^*=\dim V^*=\dim V.
$$
Therefore an injective linear map
$$
\iota : V \to (V^*)^*
$$
is automatically surjective. So $\iota$ is an isomorphism, and every element of $(V^*)^*$ is of the form
$$
f\mapsto f(x)
$$
for a unique $x\in V$. Thus
$$
V\cong (V^*)^*.
$$

(b)

Identify $(V^*)^*$ with $V$ via the canonical isomorphism above.

Let
$$
\phi_1,\dots,\phi_n
$$
be a basis of $V^*$. Then there exists a unique dual basis
$$
\Psi_1,\dots,\Psi_n
$$
of $(V^*)^*$ such that
$$
\Psi_i(\phi_j)=\delta_{ij}.
$$
By the identification $(V^*)^*\cong V$, for each $i$ there exists a unique vector $v_i\in V$ such that
$$
\Psi_i(f)=f(v_i)\qquad \text{for all } f\in V^*.
$$
Hence
$$
\phi_j(v_i)=\Psi_i(\phi_j)=\delta_{ij}.
$$
So $\phi_1,\dots,\phi_n$ is the dual basis of $v_1,\dots,v_n$.

It remains to show that $v_1,\dots,v_n$ is a basis of $V$. If
$$
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i v_i=0,
$$
then applying $\phi_j$ gives
$$
0=\phi_j\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i v_i\right)=\sum_{i=1}^n a_i\phi_j(v_i)=a_j.
$$
Thus all $a_j=0$, so $v_1,\dots,v_n$ are linearly independent. Since there are $n=\dim V$ of them, they form a basis of $V$.

(c)

Let
$$
\phi : V\to V
$$
be linear. We show that under the canonical identification $V\cong (V^*)^*$, the map $(\phi^*)^*$ corresponds to $\phi$.

Take $x\in V$, identified with $\iota(x)\in (V^*)^*$, where
$$
\iota(x)(f)=f(x).
$$
Then for every $f\in V^*$,
$$
((\phi^*)^*\iota(x))(f)
=\iota(x)(\phi^*f)
=(\phi^*f)(x)
=f(\phi x).
$$
But the functional
$$
f\mapsto f(\phi x)
$$
is exactly $\iota(\phi x)$. Hence
$$
(\phi^*)^*(\iota(x))=\iota(\phi x),
$$
which means that $(\phi^*)^*$ corresponds to $\phi$.

(d)

Assume that
$$
\phi^2=\phi
$$
and let
$$
f\in (\ker\phi)^\perp.
$$
Recall that
$$
(\ker\phi)^\perp=\{g\in V^*: g(u)=0 \text{ for all } u\in \ker\phi\}.
$$
We want to prove that
$$
\phi^*f=f.
$$

By definition of the dual map,
$$
(\phi^*f)(v)=f(\phi v)
$$
for every $v\in V$. Therefore
$$
\phi^*f=f
\iff
f(\phi v)=f(v)\ \text{for all } v\in V
\iff
f((\phi-I)v)=0\ \text{for all } v\in V.
$$
So it is enough to show that
$$
\operatorname{Im}(\phi-I)\subseteq \ker\phi.
$$

This follows immediately from $\phi^2=\phi$, because for every $v\in V$,
$$
\phi((\phi-I)v)=\phi^2v-\phi v=\phi v-\phi v=0.
$$
Hence
$$
(\phi-I)v\in \ker\phi,
$$
so indeed
$$
\operatorname{Im}(\phi-I)\subseteq \ker\phi.
$$

Now $f\in (\ker\phi)^\perp$, so $f$ vanishes on $\ker\phi$, hence also on $\operatorname{Im}(\phi-I)$. Therefore
$$
f((\phi-I)v)=0
$$
for all $v\in V$. Equivalently,
$$
f(\phi v)=f(v)
$$
for all $v\in V$. Thus
$$
(\phi^*f)(v)=f(\phi v)=f(v)
$$
for every $v\in V$, and hence
$$
\phi^*f=f.
$$

### Remark

The key observation in part (d) is
$$
\phi^2=\phi
\Longrightarrow
\phi(\phi-I)=0
\Longrightarrow
\operatorname{Im}(\phi-I)\subseteq \ker\phi.
$$
Then $f\in (\ker\phi)^\perp$ implies
$$
f\circ(\phi-I)=0,
$$
which is exactly the statement
$$
\phi^*f=f.

### Remark: Symplectic Vector Spaces

A **symplectic vector space** is a pair $(V, \omega)$, where $V$ is a finite-dimensional vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ (usually $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$), and $\omega: V \times V \to \mathbb{F}$ is a bilinear form satisfying:
1. **Skew-symmetry (Alternating):** For all $x, y \in V$, $\omega(x, y) = -\omega(y, x)$. This implies $\omega(x, x) = 0$ for all $x \in V$.
2. **Non-degeneracy:** If $\omega(x, y) = 0$ for all $y \in V$, then $x = 0$.

> **Dimension Invariant:** A fundamental theorem states that any finite-dimensional symplectic vector space must be **even-dimensional** ($\dim V = 2m$).

---

#### 1. Symplectic Basis

In a Euclidean space, we work with orthonormal bases where vectors are mutually orthogonal. In a symplectic space, vectors cannot be orthogonal to themselves since $\omega(x,x) = 0$. Instead, we look for a basis grouped into **interlocking pairs**.

Let $\dim V = 2m$. A basis of $V$ ordered and named as:
$$\{e_1, \dots, e_m, f_1, \dots, f_m\}$$
is called a **symplectic basis** if it satisfies the following structural relations for all $1 \leqslant i, j \leqslant m$:

* $\omega(e_i, e_j) = 0$
* $\omega(f_i, f_j) = 0$
* $\omega(e_i, f_j) = \delta_{ij}$

#### 2. Matrix Representation
Under a symplectic basis, the structural matrix (metric matrix) $J$ of the bilinear form $\omega$ takes the highly standard block-diagonal form:
$$J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_m \\ -I_m & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
Geometrically, a symplectic basis splits the $2m$-dimensional space into $m$ independent 2D planes spanned by each pair $(e_i, f_i)$, where $\omega(e_i, f_i) = 1$ represents a preserved signed area metric.

---

### 3. Symplectic Transformation

Analogous to orthogonal transformations (like rotations or reflections) which preserve the Euclidean inner product, a **symplectic transformation** is a linear operator that preserves the symplectic form.

A linear transformation $\phi: V \to V$ is called a **symplectic map (or symplectic transformation)** if for all $x, y \in V$:
$$\omega(\phi(x), \phi(y)) = \omega(x, y)$$

#### Matrix Representation
Let $M$ be the matrix representation of $\phi$ under a symplectic basis. According to the transformation laws for bilinear forms, $\phi$ is a symplectic transformation if and only if:
$$M^T J M = J$$
Matrices satisfying this equation belong to the **Symplectic Group**, denoted by $\text{Sp}(2m, \mathbb{R})$.
### A Key lemma
### Lemma: Isomorphism Induced by a Non-Degenerate Bilinear Form

Let $U$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$, and let $g$ be a non-degenerate bilinear form on $U$. Define a map $\mathcal{L}: U \to U^*$ by:
$$\mathcal{L}(x) = g(\cdot, x)$$
which means for any $u \in U$, $[\mathcal{L}(x)](u) = g(u, x)$. Then $\mathcal{L}$ is a linear isomorphism.

#### Proof:

1. **Well-definedness ($\mathcal{L}(x) \in U^*$):**
   For a fixed $x \in U$, we must check if $\mathcal{L}(x)$ is a valid linear functional on $U$. For any scalars $c_1, c_2 \in \mathbb{F}$ and vectors $u_1, u_2 \in U$:
   $$[\mathcal{L}(x)](c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2) = g(c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2, x)$$
   Since $g$ is bilinear, it is linear in its first argument:
   $$= c_1 g(u_1, x) + c_2 g(u_2, x) = c_1 [\mathcal{L}(x)](u_1) + c_2 [\mathcal{L}(x)](u_2)$$
   Thus, $\mathcal{L}(x)$ is a linear functional, so $\mathcal{L}(x) \in U^*$.

2. **Linearity of the Map $\mathcal{L}$:**
   We need to show that $\mathcal{L}$ itself is a linear operator from $U$ to $U^*$. Let $k_1, k_2 \in \mathbb{F}$ and $x_1, x_2 \in U$. Evaluating the functional $\mathcal{L}(k_1 x_1 + k_2 x_2)$ on any test vector $u \in U$:
   $$[\mathcal{L}(k_1 x_1 + k_2 x_2)](u) = g(u, k_1 x_1 + k_2 x_2)$$
   By the bilinearity of $g$ (linearity in the second argument):
   $$= k_1 g(u, x_1) + k_2 g(u, x_2) = k_1 [\mathcal{L}(x_1)](u) + k_2 [\mathcal{L}(x_2)](u)$$
   $$= [k_1 \mathcal{L}(x_1) + k_2 \mathcal{L}(x_2)](u)$$
   Since this holds for all $u \in U$, we conclude that:
   $$\mathcal{L}(k_1 x_1 + k_2 x_2) = k_1 \mathcal{L}(x_1) + k_2 \mathcal{L}(x_2)$$
   Hence, $\mathcal{L}$ is a linear map.

3. **Injectivity:**
   To show $\mathcal{L}$ is injective, we analyze its kernel. Suppose $x_0 \in \ker(\mathcal{L})$, meaning $\mathcal{L}(x_0) = 0$ (the zero functional in $U^*$). Then for all $u \in U$:
   $$[\mathcal{L}(x_0)](u) = 0 \implies g(u, x_0) = 0 \quad (\forall u \in U)$$
   Since the bilinear form $g$ is given to be **non-degenerate**, the only vector orthogonal to the entire space is the zero vector. Therefore, we must have:
   $$x_0 = 0$$
   This implies $\ker(\mathcal{L}) = \{0\}$, proving that $\mathcal{L}$ is injective.

4. **Bijectivity (Isomorphism):**
   By the rank-nullity theorem, since $\mathcal{L}$ is injective, the dimension of its image equals the dimension of the domain:
   $$\dim(\text{Im}(\mathcal{L})) = \dim U$$
   For any finite-dimensional vector space, a fundamental theorem of dual spaces states that $\dim U^* = \dim U$. Thus:
   $$\dim(\text{Im}(\mathcal{L})) = \dim U^*$$
   Because the image is a subspace of $U^*$ with the same finite dimension, $\mathcal{L}$ must be surjective as well. 

As a bijective linear map, $\mathcal{L}: U \to U^*$ is a linear isomorphism. $\blacksquare$

2. **Symmetry:** Notice that:
   $$g_s(y, x) = \frac{g(y, x) + g(x, y)}{2} = g_s(x, y)$$
   Thus, $g_s$ is a symmetric bilinear form.
3. **Alternating Property:** Notice that:
   $$g_a(y, x) = \frac{g(y, x) - g(x, y)}{2} = -\frac{g(x, y) - g(y, x)}{2} = -g_a(x, y)$$
   Additionally, $g_a(x, x) = \frac{g(x,x) - g(x,x)}{2} = 0$. Thus, $g_a$ is an alternating (skew-symmetric) bilinear form.

Hence, $g = g_s + g_a$, showing that any scalar product can be decomposed into a symmetric and an alternating part. $\blacksquare$

---

#### (b) Condition for Non-Degeneracy on a Subspace
**Proof:**
Recall the definition of the orthogonal complement of a subspace $U$ under $g$:
$$U^\perp = \{v \in V \mid g(u, v) = 0, \, \forall u \in U\}$$
*(Note: Since $g$ is either symmetric or alternating, the left and right orthogonal complements coincide, so order does not matter).*

* **Necessity ($\implies$):**
  Assume $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$. We want to show that $g|_U$ is non-degenerate.
  Suppose there exists a vector $u_0 \in U$ such that $g(u, u_0) = 0$ for all $u \in U$. 
  By symmetry or skew-symmetry, this implies $g(u_0, u) = 0$ for all $u \in U$. By definition, this means $u_0 \in U^\perp$. 
  Since $u_0$ is already in $U$, it belongs to the intersection: $u_0 \in U \cap U^\perp$. 
  By our assumption, $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$, which forces $u_0 = 0$. Hence, $g|_U$ is non-degenerate.

* **Sufficiency ($\impliedby$):**
  Assume the restriction $g|_U$ is a non-degenerate bilinear form. We want to show that $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$.
  Let $x$ be an arbitrary vector in $U \cap U^\perp$.
  Since $x \in U^\perp$, we have $g(u, x) = 0$ for all $u \in U$. 
  Since $x \in U$, this statement means that $x$, viewed as an element of $U$, evaluates to zero against all elements of $U$ under $g|_U$. 
  By the non-degeneracy of $g|_U$, we must have $x = 0$. 
  Thus, $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$. $\blacksquare$

#### (c) Direct Sum Decomposition
**Proof:**
Under the condition $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$, to prove $V = U \oplus U^\perp$, it suffices to show that $V = U + U^\perp$. We present an explicit construction utilizing the previous **Lemma**.

Let $v \in V$ be any vector. Consider the map $f: U \to \mathbb{F}$ defined by $f(u) = g(u, v)$. Due to the bilinearity of $g$, $f$ is a well-defined linear functional on $U$, so $f \in U^*$.

Since $g|_U$ is a non-degenerate bilinear form on the finite-dimensional space $U$, the previous **Lemma** guarantees that the mapping $\mathcal{L}: U \to U^*$ defined by $\mathcal{L}(x) = g(\cdot, x)|_U$ is an isomorphism. 

Because $\mathcal{L}$ is surjective, there exists a **unique** vector $u_0 \in U$ such that:
$$\mathcal{L}(u_0) = f \implies g(u, u_0) = g(u, v) \quad (\forall u \in U)$$

Now, we can uniquely decompose $v$ as:
$$v = u_0 + (v - u_0)$$
Clearly $u_0 \in U$. Let us check if the second part $(v - u_0)$ lies in $U^\perp$. For any $u \in U$:
$$g(u, v - u_0) = g(u, v) - g(u, u_0) = 0$$
Thus, $(v - u_0) \in U^\perp$. This proves that any vector $v \in V$ can be written as the sum of a vector in $U$ and a vector in $U^\perp$. Combined with $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$, we conclude:
$$V = U \oplus U^\perp \quad \blacksquare$$

#### (d) Existence of a Symplectic Basis
**Proof:**
We prove the existence of a symplectic basis by mathematical induction on $\dim V$. Let $\omega$ be the symplectic form (non-degenerate alternating form) on $V$.

1. **Base Case:** If $\dim V = 0$, the statement is vacuously true. If $\dim V > 0$, since $\omega$ is non-degenerate and not identically zero, there must exist two vectors $e_1, f_1 \in V$ such that $\omega(e_1, f_1) = c \neq 0$. Since $\omega$ is alternating, $\omega(e_1, e_1)=0$, implying $e_1, f_1$ are linearly independent. By rescaling $f_1 \leftarrow \frac{1}{c}f_1$, we can normalize the relation to:
   $$\omega(e_1, f_1) = 1$$
2. **Inductive Step:** Let $U = \text{span}\{e_1, f_1\}$ be a $2$-dimensional subspace of $V$. Under the basis $\{e_1, f_1\}$, the matrix of $\omega|_U$ is $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, whose determinant is $1 \neq 0$. Thus, $\omega|_U$ is non-degenerate.
   By the result in **part (c)**, the space can be split into a direct sum:
   $$V = U \oplus U^\perp$$
   We claim that $\omega$ restricted to $U^\perp$ is still a symplectic form:
   * **Alternating:** Inherited naturally on the subspace.
   * **Non-degeneracy:** Suppose there is some $x \in U^\perp$ such that $\omega(x, y) = 0$ for all $y \in U^\perp$. Since $x \in U^\perp$, we also have $\omega(x, u) = 0$ for all $u \in U$. Because $V = U \oplus U^\perp$, this means $x$ is orthogonal to the entire space $V$. By the global non-degeneracy of $\omega$, we must have $x = 0$.

Thus, $(U^\perp, \omega|_{U^\perp})$ is a symplectic space of dimension $\dim V - 2$. By the induction hypothesis, $U^\perp$ admits a symplectic basis $\{e_2, \dots, e_m, f_2, \dots, f_m\}$.
Combining the basis of $U$ and the symplectic basis of $U^\perp$, we obtain a complete symplectic basis for $V$:
$$\{e_1, e_2, \dots, m, f_1, f_2, \dots, f_m\}$$
By mathematical induction, every finite-dimensional symplectic space possesses a symplectic basis. $\blacksquare$

---

#### (e) Symplectic Transformations Map Symplectic Bases to Symplectic Bases
**Proof:**
Let $\phi$ be a symplectic transformation on $V$, and let $\{e_1, \dots, e_m, f_1, \dots, f_m\}$ be a symplectic basis of $V$. We define the transformed set of vectors as:
$$e_i' = \phi(e_i), \quad f_i' = \phi(f_i) \quad (1 \leqslant i \leqslant m)$$

1. **Linear Independence:** Suppose $x \in \ker(\phi)$. Then for all $y \in V$:
   $$\omega(x, y) = \omega(\phi(x), \phi(y)) = \omega(0, \phi(y)) = 0$$
   By the non-degeneracy of $\omega$, $x = 0$, meaning $\ker(\phi) = \{0\}$. Thus, $\phi$ is injective, and on a finite-dimensional space, it is invertible (bijective). Invertible linear operators map linearly independent bases to linearly independent bases. Hence, the new set forms a basis of $V$.
2. **Preservation of Symplectic Relations:** Using the definition that $\omega(\phi(x), \phi(y)) = \omega(x, y)$, we directly compute:
   * $\omega(e_i', e_j') = \omega(\phi(e_i), \phi(e_j)) = \omega(e_i, e_j) = 0$
   * $\omega(f_i', f_j') = \omega(\phi(f_i), \phi(f_j)) = \omega(f_i, f_j) = 0$
   * $\omega(e_i', f_j') = \omega(\phi(e_i), \phi(f_j)) = \omega(e_i, f_j) = \delta_{ij}$

Therefore, the transformed basis satisfies all criteria of a symplectic basis. $\blacksquare$

#### (f) Matrix Equation and Value of Determinant
**Proof:**

1. **The Equation $P^T A P = A$:**
   Let $\phi$ be a linear transformation with matrix representation $P$ under a specific symplectic basis. Under this basis ordering, the structural matrix of the form $\omega$ is $A$. Under the action of $\phi$, the new metric matrix of the transformed basis vectors becomes $P^T A P$.
   According to **part (e)**, a symplectic transformation maps a symplectic basis to another symplectic basis, meaning the structural metric matrix must remain unchanged. Thus:
   $$P^T A P = A$$

2. **Deducing $\det(\phi) = \pm 1$:**
   Taking the determinant on both sides of the matrix equation:
   $$\det(P^T A P) = \det(A) \implies \det(P)^2 \cdot \det(A) = \det(A)$$
   The matrix $A$ is a block-diagonal matrix composed of $m$ blocks of $\mathbf{S}$. Since $\det(\mathbf{S}) = 0 - (-1) = 1$, we have $\det(A) = 1^m = 1 \neq 0$. Dividing both sides by $\det(A)$ yields:
   $$\det(P)^2 = 1 \implies \det(P) = \pm 1$$
   Since the determinant of a linear operator is independent of the choice of basis, $\det(\phi) = \pm 1$. $\blacksquare$

#### (g) Determinant of a Real Symplectic Matrix is 1
**Proof:**
We can establish that $\det(P) = 1$ using the topological properties of the Real Symplectic Group $\text{Sp}(2m, \mathbb{R})$.

The real symplectic group $\text{Sp}(2m, \mathbb{R}) = \{P \in \mathbb{R}^{2m \times 2m} \mid P^T A P = A\}$ is a **connected** topological manifold (connected Lie group). 

The algebraic determinant map $\det: \text{Sp}(2m, \mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}$ is a continuous function. Since the continuous image of a connected set must be connected, the set of all possible determinants of matrices in $\text{Sp}(2m, \mathbb{R})$ must be a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$.

From **part (f)**, we already know that the image of the determinant map can only contain discrete values from the set $\{1, -1\}$. 

Since the identity matrix $I_{2m}$ belongs to $\text{Sp}(2m, \mathbb{R})$ and $\det(I_{2m}) = 1$, the continuous image contains $1$. Because a discrete set like $\{1, -1\}$ is disconnected, it is impossible for a continuous path starting at $I_{2m}$ to jump to $-1$ without crossing values in between. 

Thus, the determinant must be constant across the entire connected component, concluding that for any real symplectic matrix $P$:
$$\det(P) = 1 \quad $$

## Problem 1: Canonical Isomorphisms of Tensor Products
Let $U, V, W$ be linear spaces over a field $K$. Let $F \cong K$ be a $K$-linear space generated by a single element $e$ (for example: $K = \mathbb{R}, F = 2\mathbb{R}$). Prove that there exist the following canonical isomorphisms of $K$-linear spaces:

### (a) Scalar Multiplication Identity
$$F \otimes_K V \to V, \quad ae \otimes x \mapsto ax$$

### (b) Commutativity (Symmetry)
$$V \otimes_K W \to W \otimes_K V, \quad x \otimes y \mapsto y \otimes x$$

### (c) Associativity
$$(U \otimes_K V) \otimes_K W \to U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W), \quad (x \otimes y) \otimes z \mapsto x \otimes (y \otimes z)$$

### Solution 1:

#### (a) Proof of $F \otimes_K V \cong V$
**Proof:**
Since $F$ is a $K$-linear space generated by a single element $e$, any element in $F$ can be uniquely expressed as $ae$ for some $a \in K$.

1. **Construct a bilinear map:**
   Define a map $\phi: F \times V \to V$ by:
   $$\phi(ae, x) = ax, \quad \forall a \in K, x \in V$$
   It is straightforward to verify that $\phi$ is $K$-bilinear. For instance, linearity in the second variable: $\phi(ae, x+y) = a(x+y) = ax+ay = \phi(ae, x) + \phi(ae, y)$.
2. **Apply the universal property:**
   By the universal property of tensor products, the bilinear map $\phi$ induces a unique $K$-linear map $\Phi: F \otimes_K V \to V$ such that:
   $$\Phi(ae \otimes x) = ax$$
3. **Construct the inverse map:**
   Define a map $\Psi: V \to F \otimes_K V$ by:
   $$\Psi(x) = e \otimes x$$
   $\Psi$ is clearly $K$-linear because the tensor product is linear in each component.
4. **Verify mutually inverse:**
   * For any $x \in V$: $(\Phi \circ \Psi)(x) = \Phi(e \otimes x) = 1 \cdot x = x$. Thus, $\Phi \circ \Psi = \text{id}_V$.
   * For any pure tensor $ae \otimes x$: $(\Psi \circ \Phi)(ae \otimes x) = \Psi(ax) = e \otimes (ax) = ae \otimes x$. By linear extension to the entire tensor product space, $\Psi \circ \Phi = \text{id}_{F \otimes V}$.

Therefore, $\Phi$ is a canonical isomorphism, and $F \otimes_K V \cong V$. $\quad\blacksquare$


#### (b) Proof of $V \otimes_K W \cong W \otimes_K V$
**Proof:**
1. **Construct a bilinear map:**
   Define $\phi: V \times W \to W \otimes_K V$ by:
   $$\phi(x, y) = y \otimes x$$
   This map is clearly $K$-bilinear.
2. **Induce a linear map:**
   By the universal property, there exists a unique $K$-linear map $\Phi: V \otimes_K W \to W \otimes_K V$ satisfying:
   $$\Phi(x \otimes y) = y \otimes x$$
3. **Construct the inverse:**
   Symmetrically, define a bilinear map $\psi: W \times V \to V \otimes_K W$ where $\psi(y, x) = x \otimes y$. This induces a linear map $\Psi: W \otimes_K V \to V \otimes_K W$ such that:
   $$\Psi(y \otimes x) = x \otimes y$$
4. **Verify mutually inverse:**
   On any pure tensor: $(\Psi \circ \Phi)(x \otimes y) = \Psi(y \otimes x) = x \otimes y$. Extending by linearity gives $\Psi \circ \Phi = \text{id}_{V \otimes W}$. Similarly, $\Phi \circ \Psi = \text{id}_{W \otimes V}$.

Thus, the spaces are canonically isomorphic. $\quad\blacksquare$



#### (c) Proof of $(U \otimes_K V) \otimes_K W \cong U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W)$
**Proof:**
1. **Construct the forward map:**
   Fix an arbitrary $z \in W$. Define a bilinear map $f_z: U \times V \to U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W)$ by:
   $$f_z(x, y) = x \otimes (y \otimes z)$$
   By the universal property, this induces a unique linear map $\tilde{f}_z: U \otimes_K V \to U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W)$ such that $\tilde{f}_z(x \otimes y) = x \otimes (y \otimes z)$.
   
   Next, define a map $g: (U \otimes_K V) \times W \to U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W)$ by letting $z$ vary:
   $$g(t, z) = \tilde{f}_z(t), \quad \forall t \in U \otimes_K V$$
   Since $x \otimes (y \otimes z)$ is linear with respect to $z$, $g$ is bilinear. Using the universal property again, $g$ induces a unique linear map $\Phi: (U \otimes_K V) \otimes_K W \to U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W)$ satisfying:
   $$\Phi((x \otimes y) \otimes z) = x \otimes (y \otimes z)$$
2. **Construct the inverse map:**
   By a completely symmetric argument, there exists a unique linear map $\Psi: U \otimes_K (V \otimes_K W) \to (U \otimes_K V) \otimes_K W$ satisfying:
   $$\Psi(x \otimes (y \otimes z)) = (x \otimes y) \otimes z$$
3. **Verify mutually inverse:**
   On pure basis tensors: $(\Psi \circ \Phi)((x \otimes y) \otimes z) = (x \otimes y) \otimes z$. By linear extension, $\Psi \circ \Phi = \text{id}$. Similarly, $\Phi \circ \Psi = \text{id}$.

Thus, associativity holds up to canonical isomorphism. $\quad\blacksquare$

## Problem 2: Tensor-Hom Adjunction and Endomorphism Isomorphism

### (a) Space of Bilinear Maps
For linear spaces $V, W, E$ over a field $K$, prove that $\text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E)$ and $\text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, E)$ are isomorphic as $K$-linear spaces.

### (b) Tensor-Hom Adjunction
Furthermore, prove that:
$$\text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V, \text{Hom}_K(W, E)) \cong \text{Hom}_K(W, \text{Hom}_K(V, E))$$
*(Note: This demonstrates that the tensor product functor and the Hom functor form an adjoint pair).*

### (c) Isomorphism of Endomorphisms
Prove that:
$$\text{Hom}_K(V, V) \otimes_K \text{Hom}_K(W, W) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, V \otimes_K W)$$



### Solution

#### (a) Proof of $\text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, E)$
**Proof:**
Let $\tau: V \times W \to V \otimes_K W$ be the canonical universal bilinear map where $\tau(x, y) = x \otimes y$.

1. **Construct $\mathcal{F}$:**
   Define $\mathcal{F}: \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, E) \to \text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E)$ by:
   $$\mathcal{F}(f) = f \circ \tau$$
   Since $f$ is linear and $\tau$ is bilinear, the composition $f \circ \tau$ is inherently a bilinear map. $\mathcal{F}$ itself is clearly linear.
2. **Construct the inverse $\mathcal{G}$:**
   Define $\mathcal{G}: \text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E) \to \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, E)$. For any bilinear map $g \in \text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E)$, the universal property of tensor products guarantees the **existence and uniqueness** of a linear map $\tilde{g}: V \otimes_K W \to E$ such that $g = \tilde{g} \circ \tau$. We define:
   $$\mathcal{G}(g) = \tilde{g}$$
3. **Verify mutually inverse:**
   * $\mathcal{G}(\mathcal{F}(f)) = \mathcal{G}(f \circ \tau)$. By uniqueness in the universal property, the only map inducing $f \circ \tau$ is $f$ itself, so $\mathcal{G}(\mathcal{F}(f)) = f$.
   * $\mathcal{F}(\mathcal{G}(g)) = \mathcal{F}(\tilde{g}) = \tilde{g} \circ \tau = g$.

Hence, the two spaces are isomorphic. $\quad\blacksquare$



#### (b) Proof of Tensor-Hom Adjunction
**Proof:**
We must show $\text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V, \text{Hom}_K(W, E))$.

1. **Construct the isomorphism $\mathcal{H}$:**
   For any bilinear map $g: V \times W \to E$, define $\mathcal{H}(g): V \to \text{Hom}_K(W, E)$ by partially applying the first variable:
   $$\left( \mathcal{H}(g)(x) \right)(y) = g(x, y)$$
   Because $g$ is linear in $y$, $\mathcal{H}(g)(x)$ is a valid linear map in $\text{Hom}_K(W, E)$. Because $g$ is linear in $x$, the map $\mathcal{H}(g)$ is linear overall.
2. **Construct the inverse:**
   For any linear map $\alpha \in \text{Hom}_K(V, \text{Hom}_K(W, E))$, define $\mathcal{H}^{-1}(\alpha): V \times W \to E$ by:
   $$\left(\mathcal{H}^{-1}(\alpha)\right)(x, y) = (\alpha(x))(y)$$
   This naturally recovers a bilinear map. These operations are clearly mutually inverse bijections.
3. **Chain of Isomorphisms:**
   Similarly, by partially applying the second variable, we get $\text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(W, \text{Hom}_K(V, E))$. Combining this with the result from (a), we conclude:
   $$\text{Bil}_K(V \times W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, E) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V, \text{Hom}_K(W, E)) \cong \text{Hom}_K(W, \text{Hom}_K(V, E))$$
   $\quad\blacksquare$



#### (c) Proof of $\text{Hom}_K(V,V) \otimes_K \text{Hom}_K(W,W) \cong \text{Hom}_K(V \otimes_K W, V \otimes_K W)$
**Proof:**
Let $\text{End}(M)$ denote $\text{Hom}_K(M, M)$. We assume $V$ and $W$ are finite-dimensional (standard for this canonical isomorphism to hold surjectively). Let $\dim V = n$ and $\dim W = m$.

1. **Construct a bilinear map:**
   Define $\phi: \text{End}(V) \times \text{End}(W) \to \text{End}(V \otimes_K W)$. For any $f \in \text{End}(V)$ and $g \in \text{End}(W)$, the assignment $(x, y) \mapsto f(x) \otimes g(y)$ is bilinear. By the universal property, this induces a unique linear map $f \otimes g \in \text{End}(V \otimes_K W)$ satisfying:
   $$(f \otimes g)(x \otimes y) = f(x) \otimes g(y)$$
   We set $\phi(f, g) = f \otimes g$, which is visibly bilinear with respect to $f$ and $g$.
2. **Induce a linear map:**
   The bilinear map $\phi$ induces a unique linear map $\Phi: \text{End}(V) \otimes_K \text{End}(W) \to \text{End}(V \otimes_K W)$ acting on pure tensors as:
   $$\Phi(f \otimes g)(x \otimes y) = f(x) \otimes g(y)$$
3. **Check dimensions:**
   * $\dim(\text{End}(V) \otimes_K \text{End}(W)) = n^2 \cdot m^2 = (nm)^2$
   * $\dim(\text{End}(V \otimes_K W)) = (nm)^2$
   Since dimensions are equal, it suffices to prove $\Phi$ is injective.
4. **Prove Injectivity:**
   Suppose $\sum_i f_i \otimes g_i \in \ker \Phi$, where $\{g_i\}$ is a linearly independent basis for $\text{End}(W)$.
   For any $x \in V$ and $y \in W$:
   $$\Phi\left(\sum_i f_i \otimes g_i\right)(x \otimes y) = \sum_i f_i(x) \otimes g_i(y) = 0$$
   Fix $x \in V$. The maps $g_i$ evaluated at $y$ essentially act on $W$. Because $\{g_i\}$ are linearly independent operators, setting $\sum_i f_i(x) \otimes g_i = 0$ implies the coefficients $f_i(x)$ must be the zero vector for all $i$.
   Since $x$ is arbitrary, $f_i = 0$ as a map for all $i$. Therefore, $\sum_i f_i \otimes g_i = 0$. The kernel is trivial, so $\Phi$ is injective.

By rank-nullity on equal-dimension spaces, $\Phi$ is an isomorphism. $\quad\blacksquare$

## Problem 3: Tensor Product and Direct Sums

### (a) Distributivity over Direct Sums
Let $\{V_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a family of $K$-linear spaces, and let $W$ be another $K$-linear space. Prove that:

### Problem 3: Tensor Product and Direct Sums

#### (a) Proof of Distributivity over Direct Sums
**Proof:**
We need to establish an isomorphism between $\left(\bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i\right) \otimes_K W$ and $\bigoplus_{i \in I} (V_i \otimes_K W)$. We will construct mutually inverse linear maps using universal properties.

1.  **Constructing the forward map $\Phi$:**
    Any element in the direct sum $\bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i$ can be uniquely written as a finite sum $\sum_{i \in I} v_i$ (where only finitely many $v_i$ are non-zero).
    Define a map $\phi: \left(\bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i\right) \times W \longrightarrow \bigoplus_{i \in I} (V_i \otimes_K W)$ by:
    $$\phi\left( \sum_{i \in I} v_i, w \right) = \sum_{i \in I} (v_i \otimes w)$$
    Because tensor products are linear in both coordinates, $\phi$ is naturally a $K$-bilinear map. 
    By the universal property of tensor products, $\phi$ induces a unique $K$-linear map $\Phi$:
    $$\Phi: \left(\bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i\right) \otimes_K W \longrightarrow \bigoplus_{i \in I} (V_i \otimes_K W)$$
    satisfying $\Phi\left( \left(\sum v_i\right) \otimes w \right) = \sum (v_i \otimes w)$.

2.  **Constructing the inverse map $\Psi$:**
    For each specific index $j \in I$, there is a natural canonical injection $\iota_j: V_j \to \bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i$. 
    This induces a linear map on the tensor product:
    $$\tilde{\iota}_j: V_j \otimes_K W \longrightarrow \left(\bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i\right) \otimes_K W, \quad v_j \otimes w \mapsto \iota_j(v_j) \otimes w$$
    By the universal property of direct sums (as coproducts), the collection of linear maps $\{\tilde{\iota}_j\}_{j \in I}$ induces a unique global linear map $\Psi$ from the direct sum:
    $$\Psi: \bigoplus_{i \in I} (V_i \otimes_K W) \longrightarrow \left(\bigoplus_{i \in I} V_i\right) \otimes_K W$$
    satisfying $\Psi(v_j \otimes w) = v_j \otimes w$ (where $v_j$ on the right side is viewed as embedded in the direct sum).

3.  **Verifying mutual inverses:**
    * $(\Psi \circ \Phi) \left( (\sum v_i) \otimes w \right) = \Psi \left( \sum (v_i \otimes w) \right) = \sum (v_i \otimes w) = (\sum v_i) \otimes w$. Thus, $\Psi \circ \Phi = \text{id}$.
    * $(\Phi \circ \Psi) (v_j \otimes w) = \Phi(v_j \otimes w) = v_j \otimes w$. By linear extension to the direct sum, $\Phi \circ \Psi = \text{id}$.

Therefore, the two spaces are canonically isomorphic. $\quad\blacksquare$

#### (b) Proof of Finite-Dimensional Basis Corollary
**Proof:**
Let $(x_i)_{i=1}^n$ be a basis for the $K$-linear space $V$. 
By the definition of a basis, every element in $V$ can be uniquely represented as a linear combination of $x_i$. Therefore, $V$ decomposes into the direct sum of $n$ one-dimensional spaces generated by each basis vector:
$$V \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^n K x_i$$

Using the distributivity property proven in **Part (a)**, we can write:
$$V \otimes_K W \cong \left(\bigoplus_{i=1}^n K x_i\right) \otimes_K W \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^n (K x_i \otimes_K W)$$

Now, consider each component $K x_i \otimes_K W$. Notice that $K x_i$ is a one-dimensional $K$-linear space generated by the single element $x_i$. 
According to the result from **Problem 1(a)** (which states that $F \otimes_K M \cong M$ for a 1D space $F$), we can substitute $F$ with $K x_i$ and $M$ with $W$ to get canonical isomorphisms:
$$K x_i \otimes_K W \cong W$$

Substituting this back into our direct sum yields:
$$V \otimes_K W \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^n (K x_i \otimes_K W) \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^n W$$

The direct sum of $n$ copies of $W$ is precisely $W^n$. 
Thus, we conclude:
$$V \otimes_K W \cong W^n$$
$\quad\blacksquare$.

# Exact Sequences and the Tensor Product Functor

This document presents the rigorous proofs for the exactness properties of the tensor product functor over a field $F$. These problems explore the concepts of right exactness, exactness of the tensor product over fields (vector spaces), and the necessary and sufficient conditions for exactness.

---

## Mathematical Preliminaries and Definitions

### 1. Exact Sequences
Let $F$ be a field, and let $U, V, W$ be vector spaces over $F$ (also referred to as $F$-linear spaces). A sequence of vector spaces and linear maps
$$\cdots \longrightarrow A_{i-1} \xrightarrow{f_{i-1}} A_i \xrightarrow{f_i} A_{i+1} \longrightarrow \cdots$$
is said to be **exact at $A_i$** if the image of the incoming map equals the kernel of the outgoing map:
$$\text{Im}(f_{i-1}) = \text{Ker}(f_i)$$
A sequence is called an **exact sequence** if it is exact at every intermediate object.
- If $A \xrightarrow{f} B \longrightarrow 0$ is exact at $B$, then $\text{Im}(f) = \text{Ker}(B \to 0) = B$, which means $f$ is **surjective**.
- If $0 \longrightarrow A \xrightarrow{g} B$ is exact at $A$, then $\text{Im}(0 \to A) = \{0\} = \text{Ker}(g)$, which means $g$ is **injective**.

### 2. Tensor Product of Linear Maps
Given an $F$-linear map $\phi: U \to V$ and any $F$-linear space $N$, the tensor product map $\phi \otimes \text{id}_N: U \otimes_F N \to V \otimes_F N$ is the unique linear map satisfying:
$$(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)(u \otimes n) = \phi(u) \otimes n \quad \forall u \in U, n \in N$$
An arbitrary element of $U \otimes_F N$ can be written as a finite sum of pure tensors, $\sum_{i} u_i \otimes n_i$. By linearity, the action of the map is given by:
$$(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)\left(\sum_{i} u_i \otimes n_i\right) = \sum_{i} \phi(u_i) \otimes n_i$$

## Problem 4

### Problem 4(a)
**Statement:**
Prove that the tensor product functor is right exact. That is, for any exact sequence of $F$-linear spaces
$$U \xrightarrow{\phi} V \xrightarrow{\psi} W \longrightarrow 0$$
show that for any $F$-linear space $N$, the corresponding sequence
$$U \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\phi \otimes \text{id}_N} V \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\psi \otimes \text{id}_N} W \otimes_F N \longrightarrow 0$$
is also exact.

**Proof:**
To establish the exactness of the transformed sequence, we must prove two conditions:
1. The map $\psi \otimes \text{id}_N$ is surjective (exactness at $W \otimes_F N$).
2. $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$ (exactness at $V \otimes_F N$).

#### Step 1: Proof of Surjectivity of $\psi \otimes \text{id}_N$
Let $y \in W \otimes_F N$ be an arbitrary element. Any element in the tensor product space can be represented as a finite sum of pure tensors:
$$y = \sum_{j=1}^k w_j \otimes n_j, \quad \text{where } w_j \in W, n_j \in N$$
Since the original sequence is exact at $W$, the map $\psi: V \to W$ is surjective. Thus, for each $w_j \in W$, there exists an element $v_j \in V$ such that $\psi(v_j) = w_j$.
Construct the element $x = \sum_{j=1}^k v_j \otimes n_j \in V \otimes_F N$. Applying the map $\psi \otimes \text{id}_N$ yields:
$$(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)(x) = (\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)\left(\sum_{j=1}^k v_j \otimes n_j\right) = \sum_{j=1}^k \psi(v_j) \otimes n_j = \sum_{j=1}^k w_j \otimes n_j = y$$
Since every element $y \in W \otimes_F N$ has a preimage $x \in V \otimes_F N$, the map $\psi \otimes \text{id}_N$ is surjective. This proves exactness at $W \otimes_F N$, meaning the sequence ends with $\longrightarrow 0$.

#### Step 2: Proof of $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) \subseteq \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$
Consider any pure tensor $u \otimes n \in U \otimes_F N$. Applying the composition of the two maps gives:
$$(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N) \circ (\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)(u \otimes n) = (\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)(\phi(u) \otimes n) = \psi(\phi(u)) \otimes n$$
Since the original sequence is exact at $V$, we know that $\text{Im}(\phi) = \text{Ker}(\psi)$, which implies $\psi \circ \phi = 0$. Therefore, $\psi(\phi(u)) = 0$ for all $u \in U$.
Thus, the expression becomes:
$$0 \otimes n = 0$$
Since the composition vanishes on all pure tensors, by linearity, it vanishes on all elements of $U \otimes_F N$. Consequently, $(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N) \circ (\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) = 0$, which implies:
$$\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) \subseteq \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$$

#### Step 3: Proof of $\text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N) \subseteq \text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)$
Let $\{n_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a basis of the $F$-linear space $N$. Any element $x \in V \otimes_F N$ can be uniquely expressed in the form:
$$x = \sum_{i \in I} v_i \otimes n_i$$
where $v_i \in V$, and all but finitely many $v_i$ are zero.
Assume that $x \in \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$. Applying $\psi \otimes \text{id}_N$ results in:
$$(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)(x) = (\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)\left(\sum_{i \in I} v_i \otimes n_i\right) = \sum_{i \in I} \psi(v_i) \otimes n_i = 0$$
In the space $W \otimes_F N$, since $\{n_i\}_{i \in I}$ forms a basis for $N$, the family of expressions $\{\cdot \otimes n_i\}$ preserves linear independence over $F$. Therefore, the sum $\sum_{i \in I} \psi(v_i) \otimes n_i = 0$ implies that each individual coefficient must vanish:
$$\psi(v_i) = 0 \quad \forall i \in I$$
This implies that $v_i \in \text{Ker}(\psi)$ for all $i \in I$.
By the exactness of the original sequence at $V$, we have $\text{Ker}(\psi) = \text{Im}(\phi)$. Thus, for each $v_i$, there exists an element $u_i \in U$ such that $\phi(u_i) = v_i$.
Substituting this back into the expression for $x$:
$$x = \sum_{i \in I} v_i \otimes n_i = \sum_{i \in I} \phi(u_i) \otimes n_i = (\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)\left(\sum_{i \in I} u_i \otimes n_i\right)$$
Since $\sum_{i \in I} u_i \otimes n_i$ is a well-defined element of $U \otimes_F N$, it follows that $x \in \text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)$. Hence, $\text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N) \subseteq \text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)$.

Combining Step 2 and Step 3, we obtain $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$, which completes the proof of right exactness. $\blacksquare$

### Problem 4(b)
**Statement:**
Prove that in fact, the tensor product functor over a field is exact. That is, given an exact sequence of $F$-linear spaces
$$U \xrightarrow{\phi} V \xrightarrow{\psi} W$$
show that for any $F$-linear space $N$, the sequence
$$U \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\phi \otimes \text{id}_N} V \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\psi \otimes \text{id}_N} W \otimes_F N$$
is exact.

**Proof:**
The exactness of the sequence $U \xrightarrow{\phi} V \xrightarrow{\psi} W$ means that $\text{Im}(\phi) = \text{Ker}(\psi)$. We need to show that $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$.

Let us re-examine the proofs of **Step 2** and **Step 3** from Problem 4(a):
- In **Step 2**, we proved $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) \subseteq \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$ by using only the fact that $\psi \circ \phi = 0$ (i.e., $\text{Im}(\phi) \subseteq \text{Ker}(\psi)$). The surjectivity of $\psi$ was never utilized.
- In **Step 3**, we proved $\text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N) \subseteq \text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N)$ by choosing a basis $\{n_i\}$ for $N$, expanding an arbitrary element of the kernel, showing that its components lie in $\text{Ker}(\psi)$, and then lifting them to $U$ via the property $\text{Ker}(\psi) \subseteq \text{Im}(\phi)$. At no point did this step require $\psi$ to be surjective.

Since both inclusions hold true independently of the surjectivity of $\psi$, the relation $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$ holds. Thus, the sequence is exact at the intermediate object $V \otimes_F N$. $\blacksquare$

### Problem 4(c)
**Statement:**
Prove that for $F$-linear spaces, the sequence
$$U \xrightarrow{\phi} V \xrightarrow{\psi} W$$
is exact if and only if for any $F$-linear space $N$, the sequence
$$U \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\phi \otimes \text{id}_N} V \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\psi \otimes \text{id}_N} W \otimes_F N$$
is exact.

**Proof:**
This is an "if and only if" statement, requiring us to prove both directions.

#### Direction 1: Necessity ($\implies$)
Assume that the original sequence $U \xrightarrow{\phi} V \xrightarrow{\psi} W$ is exact. By the conclusion proved in **Problem 4(b)**, it directly follows that for any $F$-linear space $N$, the sequence $U \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\phi \otimes \text{id}_N} V \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\psi \otimes \text{id}_N} W \otimes_F N$ is exact.

#### Direction 2: Sufficiency ($\impliedby$)
Assume that for *any* $F$-linear space $N$, the sequence
$$U \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\phi \otimes \text{id}_N} V \otimes_F N \xrightarrow{\psi \otimes \text{id}_N} W \otimes_F N$$
is exact. This means that the equality $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_N) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_N)$ is guaranteed to hold for every choice of $N$.

Since this statement holds universally for all $F$-linear spaces, we can choose a specific space for $N$ to extract information about the original maps. Let us choose $N = F$, the field itself viewed as a 1-dimensional vector space over $F$.

For any vector space $M$ over $F$, there exists a canonical vector space isomorphism:
$$\tau_M: M \otimes_F F \xrightarrow{\cong} M, \quad m \otimes c \mapsto cm$$
The inverse mapping is given by $\tau_M^{-1}(m) = m \otimes 1$.

Setting $N = F$ in our hypothesis, the sequence
$$U \otimes_F F \xrightarrow{\phi \otimes \text{id}_F} V \otimes_F F \xrightarrow{\psi \otimes \text{id}_F} W \otimes_F F$$
is exact, meaning $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_F) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_F)$.

Consider the following commutative diagram linking the tensor sequence to the original sequence via the canonical isomorphisms:
$$\begin{CD}
U \otimes_F F @>{\phi \otimes \text{id}_F}>> V \otimes_F F @>{\psi \otimes \text{id}_F}>> W \otimes_F F \\
@V{\tau_U}VV @V{\tau_V}VV @V{\tau_W}VV \\
U @>{\phi}>> V @>{\psi}>> W
\end{CD}$$

Let us check the commutativity for the first square. For any $u \otimes c \in U \otimes_F F$:
$$\tau_V \circ (\phi \otimes \text{id}_F)(u \otimes c) = \tau_V(\phi(u) \otimes c) = c\phi(u)$$
$$\phi \circ \tau_U(u \otimes c) = \phi(cu) = c\phi(u)$$
Since the diagrams commute and the vertical arrows are isomorphisms, the properties of the kernels and images are perfectly preserved under these maps. Explicitly:
$$\text{Im}(\phi) = \tau_V\left(\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_F)\right)$$
$$\text{Ker}(\psi) = \tau_V\left(\text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_F)\right)$$
Since the top sequence is exact, we know $\text{Im}(\phi \otimes \text{id}_F) = \text{Ker}(\psi \otimes \text{id}_F)$. Applying the isomorphism $\tau_V$ to both sides yields:
$$\text{Im}(\phi) = \text{Ker}(\psi)$$
Therefore, the original sequence $U \xrightarrow{\phi} V \xrightarrow{\psi} W$ is exact. $\blacksquare$

### Important Note on General Rings and Modules
As stated in the problem note, **properties (b) and (c) do not hold for general rings and modules**. 

1. **Failure of Left Exactness for Modules:** For a general ring $R$ and $R$-modules, the tensor product functor $-\otimes_R N$ is only guaranteed to be **right exact**. It is generally *not* left exact, meaning it does not preserve injectivity. For example, consider the ring $R = \mathbb{Z}$. The sequence $0 \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times 2} \mathbb{Z}$ is exact (multiplication by 2 is injective). However, tensoring with $N = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ yields:
   $$0 \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z} \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{(\times 2) \otimes \text{id}} \mathbb{Z} \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \implies 0 \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times 2} \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$
   Since multiplication by 2 on $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ maps everything to $0$, the map becomes the zero map, which is *not* injective. Hence, exactness fails on the left.
2. **Why it Works for Fields:** The proofs for (b) and (c) heavily rely on the fact that **every vector space over a field has a basis** (i.e., every module over a field is a *free module*). Free modules and more generally *flat modules* preserve exactness under tensoring. Over a field, all modules are free, hence all modules are flat, which guarantees that the tensor product functor is perfectly exact.


### Remark:Definition of an Exact Sequence

In homological algebra, an **exact sequence** is a sequence of algebraic objects (such as groups, modules, or vector spaces) and morphisms (homomorphisms) between them, such that the image of one morphism is strictly equal to the kernel of the subsequent morphism.

#### Formal Definition

Consider a sequence of objects $A_i$ and morphisms $f_i$:

$$\cdots \longrightarrow A_{i-1} \xrightarrow{f_{i-1}} A_i \xrightarrow{f_i} A_{i+1} \longrightarrow \cdots$$

This sequence is said to be **exact at $A_i$** if:

$$\text{Im}(f_{i-1}) = \text{Ker}(f_i)$$

The entire sequence is called **exact** if it is exact at every intermediate object.

#### Important Special Cases

1.  **Injective Map:** A sequence $0 \longrightarrow A \xrightarrow{f} B$ is exact at $A$ if and only if $f$ is an injective homomorphism ($\text{Ker}(f) = 0$).
2.  **Surjective Map:** A sequence $A \xrightarrow{g} B \longrightarrow 0$ is exact at $B$ if and only if $g$ is a surjective homomorphism ($\text{Im}(g) = B$).
3.  **Short Exact Sequence:** A sequence combining the above properties, typically written as:
    $$0 \longrightarrow A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C \longrightarrow 0$$
    This sequence is exact if $f$ is injective, $g$ is surjective, and $\text{Im}(f) = \text{Ker}(g)$.
